💥 Why "Monster" and Not "Hero" or "God"?

Previous analyses often default to broad Jungian interpretations. This is insufficient. The metaphor must be constrained by the specific experience of playing the chart.

"It's wrong, it's wrong, I don't want to live in a world like this, but…"
"It's wrong, it's wrong, yet still, I wish to remain myself."

A "hero" operates within social frameworks; a "god" transcends them. A monster exists outside the framework while remaining physically present within it. The chart mirrors this exactly: the player is forced to abandon normal pattern-recognition strategies (the "social contract" of rhythm games) and operate on pure reactive instinct. You are no longer "playing a song." You are imposing your continued existence onto the note stream through sheer physical persistence. The destruction is not of the chart—it is of your own cognitive limitations.

🎼 BPM 220 as Existential Threshold

At 220 BPM, 16th-note streams reach 14.7 inputs per second—within 10% of documented human tapping limits. But the number alone isn't the point. The absence of respite at this tempo creates a unique physiological state: the player's heart rate synchronizes to the kick drum, and failure occurs not from missing a complex pattern but from cumulative micro-delays in finger recovery. This is why Kaijuu feels like "becoming a monster": you are no longer consciously controlling your fingers; you are witnessing your body's limits in real time.

🔬 Sakuzyo's Design Philosophy: From Arcade to PJSK

Though "Kaijuu ni Naritai" is Sakuzyo's first Project SEKAI track, his approach to difficulty design has been honed across multiple rhythm game platforms. His earlier works—such as "Altale" (BPM 83-180, known for deceptive slowness) and "Destr0yer" (BPM 205, a chaos of trills and bursts)—demonstrate a consistent philosophy: difficulty should emerge from sustained tension rather than isolated spikes.

In "Altale," the challenge lies in maintaining precision at unnervingly low speeds. In "Destr0yer," the player is bombarded with rapid-fire trills that leave no room for recalibration. "Aleph-0" layers complex polyrhythms atop a driving BPM 250 core. Across all these works, Sakuzyo's signature is not complexity for its own sake, but the systematic removal of recovery windows. "Kaijuu ni Naritai" is the purest distillation of this principle yet: no tempo shifts, no breakdown sections, no mercy.

✨ Conclusion: A Chart That Redefines Player Agency

"Kaijuu ni Naritai" achieves what few rhythm game charts attempt: it uses tempo, density, and structural absence of rest to temporarily overwrite the player's cognitive relationship to the interface. It doesn't just ask for faster fingers—it demands a different mode of being. By comparing it directly to its peers, decomposing its sectional demands, and constraining its central metaphor to the specific experience of play, we arrive at a defensible conclusion: this is not merely a difficult chart. It is an engineered transformation.
