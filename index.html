Commissioned for the Project SEKAI Championship 2025 finals and added to the game on November 23, 2025, Sakuzyo's "Kaijuu ni Naritai" didn't just break records—it rewrote the definition of what a rhythm game chart can demand. Every single difficulty tier received a new maximum level upon release. That's not coincidence. That's a statement of intent. BPM 220. 2 minutes and 5 seconds of relentless assault. And a chart philosophy that Sakuzyo himself made explicit: "Become a monster and destroy the falling notes."

Most players see the level number and assume it's just "the hardest chart." That's surface-level. Today we're breaking down exactly what makes this track a structural masterpiece—and why that matters.

📊 The Numbers: Quantifying "Record-Breaking"

Let's establish the baseline. When people say this chart is "hard," they usually mean it feels hard. Let's talk about what it actually is.

📊 MASTER Lv.35

1,900 notes across 125 seconds = 15.2 notes/second average. BPM 220 translates to approximately 458 beats over the full runtime. This alone is stamina-draining, full-arm territory.

📊 APPEND Lv.38

2,800 notes across 125 seconds = 22.4 notes/second average. With 440 beats in the track, that's roughly 6.4 notes per beat. This is not a chart. It's an endurance sport.

But raw density is only the foundation. The APPEND chart layers multiple difficulty vectors simultaneously: high-speed trills that test finger independence, cross-hand patterns that force spatial recalibration mid-song, and chord streams that systematically drain stamina. Each element is designed to break a different aspect of your technique. It's not just "hard." It's systematic.

⚡ Sakuzyo's own words: "I poured everything into this track. Players—become monsters and destroy the notes raining down on you." The chart isn't testing you. It's demanding transformation.

💥 The Contradiction: Why "Being a Monster" Cuts Deep

The narrator—voiced by Hatsune Miku—isn't telling a tidy story. She's confessing from the edge of collapse.

"It's wrong, it's wrong, I don't want to live in a world like this, but…"
"It's wrong, it's wrong, yet still, I wish to remain myself."

This is ambivalence crystallized. The desire to destroy coexists with the refusal to disappear. "I want to be a monster" isn't about power fantasy. It's about the desperate wish to leave a scar on a world that would otherwise erase you. The narrator hates the world, hates herself, yet clings to identity with bleeding fingers. That contradiction—that tension—is what makes the track resonate beyond the arcade cabinet.

🧠 Jungian Shadow Work at 220 BPM

From a psychological perspective, this is textbook Shadow projection. The "monster" represents everything we repress to function in society: raw aggression, the need to be acknowledged, the parts of ourselves we're taught are too ugly to show. Sakuzyo gave that shadow a voice—and a BPM that forces your body to inhabit it. When you play the APPEND chart, you're not just tapping notes. You're temporarily becoming the monster, channeling that repressed energy through your fingertips. The exhaustion isn't frustration. It's catharsis.

⚙️ Structural Breakdown: BPM 220 as Narrative Device

The tempo isn't arbitrary. BPM 220 sits at the upper edge of what human perception can process as "rhythm" rather than "noise." It's fast enough to feel urgent, dangerous, perpetually on the verge of collapse—but just slow enough to remain playable. This is the musical equivalent of a monster barely contained.

The composition layers dense piano arpeggios, distorted bass frequencies, and rapid-fire percussion into a wall of sound that mirrors the narrator's internal state: overwhelming, chaotic, and straining against its own structural limits. And the chart? It doesn't just accompany that chaos. It forces you to survive it. Every trill, every cross-hand pattern, every chord stream is a demand: keep up, or get crushed.

✨ Conclusion: The Scar Remains

"Kaijuu ni Naritai" won't fix you. It won't make you stronger. What it does—through its chart design, its tempo, its lyrical ambivalence—is stand next to you in the dark and validate the part of you that wants to roar. For 2 minutes and 5 seconds, it gives you permission to be the monster. And for a track that demands 2,800 notes from your fingers, that's more than enough.
